What is the Critical Entities Resilience (CER) Directive?

The Critical Entities Resilience Directive (CER Directive) is a European Union initiative designed to address the vulnerabilities of an increasingly disrupted and interconnected world. Its goal is to enhance the resilience of critical entities against diverse threats, including natural disasters, cyberattacks, terrorism, and sabotage.

EU Member States will adopt a risk-based methodology to identify and designate critical entities—organizations deemed essential for maintaining vital economic and societal functions—across eleven key sectors.

These entities must assess the risks that could impact their ability to deliver essential services and implement appropriate resilience measures. These measures will include developing resilience plans and establishing robust procedures for incident reporting. In each Member State, designated authorities will oversee the proper implementation and enforcement of the Directive, including the imposition of penalties for non-compliance.

Why Does the CER Directive Matter?

Resilience is Here to Stay

Resilience has become a cornerstone of regulatory frameworks, with the CER Directive marking the latest step in the EU's growing focus on strengthening resilience across sectors. While earlier regulations, such as the NIS 2 Directive, primarily addressed cyber threats, the CER Directive recognizes the broader spectrum of increasingly frequent, complex, and diverse risks.

Expanding Sectoral Scope

One of the key distinguishing features of the CER Directive is its extensive sectoral coverage compared to other recent regulations, such as the Digital Operational Resilience Act (DORA). While sectors like finance and digital infrastructure may already have frameworks in place from previous regulatory initiatives, others may face resilience requirements for the first time.

Urgent Timelines

The Directive introduces stringent requirements for risk assessment and resilience planning. Although critical entities are not expected to be formally designated until July 2026, they will then have only ten months to achieve compliance. If your organization is likely to be classified as a critical entity, it is essential to begin preparations immediately.

A Strategic Opportunity

The CER Directive also opens the door for organizations to adopt a proactive, strategic approach to resilience. Beyond safeguarding value, this approach can drive competitive advantages by uncovering operational efficiencies and leveraging disruptions to innovate.

What Actions Should You Take Now?

1

Consider if You Are Likely to Be a Critical Entity

Determine if your organisation is likely to be recognised as a critical entity under the Directive based on your sector and potential impact of disruptive events.

2

Get Familiar with the CER Directive's Requirements

Familiarise yourself with the CER Directive's specific requirements, including risk assessments, resilience plans, incident notification and mitigation measures.

3

Review, Refresh and Transform Your Plan for Resilience

  • Identify and Evaluate Your Risks: Carry out comprehensive assessments to identify potential risks, understand their likely impacts, and evaluate how they align with your organization's risk tolerance.
  • Optimize Your Target Operating Model: Examine your governance structures to ensure they support a cohesive, data-driven operating model that integrates resilience into your enterprise framework.
  • Focus on Resilience for What Matters Most: Determine your organization's core priorities and build resilience around them.
  • Strengthen Crisis Management: Evaluate and update your incident and crisis management structures to handle unplanned or unprecedented events effectively.
  • Conduct Routine Training and Exercises: Implement a regular program of training, testing, and simulation exercises based on realistic scenarios.
4

Stay Up-to-Date on the CER Directive

Stay informed about updates to the CER Directive to anticipate and prepare for applicable requirements and deadlines. Even if your organization is not directly designated as a critical entity, assess whether you could serve as a supplier to one.
